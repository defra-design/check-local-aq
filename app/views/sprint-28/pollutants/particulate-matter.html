Particulate matter are small solid or liquid particles in the air. These particles can come from natural processes, industrial processes and other human activity.

There are 2 main types of particulate matter (PM):

  • PM2.5 are particles 2.5 micrometres wide, or smaller
  • PM10 are particles 10 micrometres wide, or smaller (this includes PM2.5)

For context, the width of a human hair is about 50 to 70 micrometres.

These particles are small enough for us to breathe in. When there is a lot of particulate matter in the air, this can cause lung inflammation and other health effects. The particles can also enter the bloodstream through the lungs and travel around the body, affecting the heart, brain and other organs.

Sources of particulate matter

Particulate matter includes smoke, dust, soot and small drops of liquid. These particles can come from many different sources.

The main sources of particulate matter pollution are:

  • fuel burning by cars and other vehicles
  • fuel burning by industry and domestic properties
  • vehicle tyres and brakes getting worn down
  • dust and soil from construction sites, landfills, and agriculture
  • sea spray particles
  • burning vegetation and wildfires
  • pollen

Monitoring levels of particulate matter

Particulate matter pollution is monitored across the UK.

PM2.5 and PM10 are 2 of the 5 pollutants used in the DAQI scale. The DAQI scale helps explain the short term risk of air pollution. It measures low, moderate, high and very high levels of pollutants in the air.

Pollutant levels are measured in micrograms per cubic metre (µg/m³).

Measuring PM2.5

PM2.5 pollution is considered:

  • low from 0 to 35 µg/m³
  • moderate from 36 to 53 µg/m³
  • high from 54 to 70 µg/m³
  • very high at 71 µg/m³ or higher
