Nitrogen dioxide is a brown gas that comes from burning fuels.

Breathing in a lot of nitrogen dioxide irritates the lungs. This can cause inflammation and breathing difficulties. People with existing lung conditions, like asthma, are particularly affected by nitrogen dioxide.

Health effects of nitrogen dioxide

Short term exposure to nitrogen dioxide can cause:

  • inflammation of the airways
  • asthma attacks
  • higher risk of getting respiratory infections
  • worsening symptoms of lung or heart conditions
  • worse symptoms of exposure to allergens

Long term exposure to nitrogen dioxide may lead to an overall increased risk of respiratory infection, and poorer lung function in children.

Where nitrogen dioxide comes from

Nitrogen dioxide is released when fuels are burned, for example in vehicle engines and industrial processes.

Short term high levels of nitrogen dioxide be caused by lots of road traffic, roadworks or traffic jams. Diesel fuelled vehicles usually emit far more nitrogen dioxide than equivalent petrol fuelled vehicles.

Nitrogen dioxide air pollution is often worse in urban areas, where there are more roads and more traffic. But there can also be a lot of nitrogen dioxide in busier rural areas, and the wind can blow the gas across long distances.

How nitrogen dioxide is measured

Find out how air pollution is measured and predicted.

Nitrogen dioxide pollution is considered:

  • low from 0 to 200 micrograms per cubic metre (µg/m³)
  • moderate from 201 to 400 µg/m³
  • high from 401 to 600 µg/m³
  • very high at 601 µg/m³ or higher
