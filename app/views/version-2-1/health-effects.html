Poor air quality is one of the largest environmental risks to public health in the UK.

It is a particular threat to vulnerable groups, including adults and children with lung or heart conditions and older people.

Every year in the UK, an estimated 20,000 people are admitted to hospital following high air pollution episodes. Many more develop serious health conditions after long term exposure to air pollution, even at low levels. This has a huge impact on our NHS and other public services.

How air pollution impacts the body

Air pollution (particles and gases) can damage our cells in different ways.

Pollutants usually enter our lungs first as we breathe them in. They can then enter our blood and reach other organs, such as the heart and brain. Damage to these major organs can cause many serious health conditions.

Any amount of pollution can be damaging to our health, but the more pollution you are exposed to, the greater the risk and the bigger the impact. The damage can be gradual and you may not notice it for many years. 

Although there is no safe level of air pollution, there are actions you can take to reduce your exposure.

Short term exposure to air pollution can also worsen the symptoms of existing health conditions. This includes lung conditions like asthma and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), and heart conditions.

Speak to your doctor or nurse if:

  • you have new symptoms
  • your symptoms get worse
  • your symptoms do not get better after a week

Long term air pollution exposure

Exposure to air pollution over many years increases the risk of developing a serious health condition, even when pollution levels are low.

When air pollutants enter the body, they can harm many different organs and systems, not just the respiratory system. One of the main health effects of air pollution is damage to the heart and blood vessels. There is also evidence linking air pollution to decreased brain function and conditions like dementia.
