Fine particulate matter (PM2.5)

Particulate matter are small solid or liquid particles in the air. These particles can come from natural processes, industrial processes and other human activity.

Particulate matter includes dust. soot. smoke and small drops of liquid. These particles can come from many different sources.

The main sources of PM2.5 are:

  • burning of fuel by vehicles, industry and domestic properties 
  • wear of tyres and brakes
  • wind blown soil and dust
  • sea spray particles
  • burning vegetation

Learn more about fine particulate matter (PM2.5)

Yearly levels

Annual average PM2.5 levels must not go above 20 µg/m3 in a calendar year.

The unit µg/m3 stands for micrograms (one millionth of a gram) per cubic metre of air.

Particulate matter (PM10)

Particulate matter are small solid or liquid particles in the air. These particles can come from natural processes, industrial processes and other human activity.

Particulate matter includes dust. soot. smoke and small drops of liquid. These particles can come from many different sources.

The main sources of PM10 are:

  • dust from construction sites  
  • dust from landfills
  • dust from agriculture
  • wildfires
  • pollen
  • power plants
  • vehicles

Learn more about particulate matter (PM10)

Yearly levels

Nitrogen dioxide (NO2)

Nitrogen dioxide is a colourless gas. It's mainly produced during:

  • burning of petrol or diesel in a car engine 
  • burning natural gas in a central-heating boiler or power station welding
  • the use of explosives 
  • commercial manufacturing
  • food manufacturing

Learn more about nitrogen dioxide (NO2)

Yearly levels

Annual average nitrogen dioxide levels must not go above 40 µg/m3 in a calendar year.

The unit µg/m3 stands for micrograms (one millionth of a gram) per cubic metre of air.

Ozone

Ozone is a gas that can be found in the atmosphere and at ground level.

There are no major sources of pollution that emit ozone directly. Ozone is created by chemical reactions between sunlight and other pollutants, called precursor chemicals.

Many sources can release precursor chemicals, including vehicles, industrial processes, burning fossil fuels and natural sources like plants and animals.

Ozone levels are usually higher:

  • later in the day, when there is more sunlight
  • when there are higher levels of precursor chemicals
  • in more rural areas, rather than urban areas
