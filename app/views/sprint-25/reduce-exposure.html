There is no safe level of air pollution. But there are actions you can take to reduce your exposure.

You should try to reduce your exposure to air pollution where you can, even when levels are low.

Long term exposure to air pollution (over years) can lead to many different health conditions and reduce life expectancy.

Urban areas

Urban areas have a population of more than 10,000 residents. For example, a large town or a city.

As well as the actions listed under all areas, there are specific actions you can take if you live or work in an urban area. Consider the following actions:

  • where possible adapt your travel to avoid the busiest roads at the busiest times
  • re-route travel and exercise to parks and green spaces, if you are not affected by pollen
  • if you need to walk alongside a busy road, keep as far from the edge of the road as you can - putting even a short distance between yourself and the source of pollution reduces the pollution you breathe in
  • avoid driving in heavy congestion - sitting in traffic can expose you to high levels of pollution
  • check pollution forecasts to help you plan outdoor activities

Rural areas

Rural areas have a population of less than 10,000 residents. For example, a hamlet, village or small town.

There are specific actions you can take if you live or work in a rural area. Consider the following actions:

  • check for agricultural activities - ploughing, spreading fertilisers, and pesticide spraying may contribute to pollution levels
  • avoid bonfires or burning garden waste
  • check pollution forecasts - pollution can travel long distances and you cannot always see the sources.

Some pollutants are higher in the countryside - for example, ozone.

All outdoor areas

There are actions you can take regardless of where you live or work. Consider the following actions in any area:

  • take routes where there is less traffic
  • try to avoid heavily trafficked areas when they are at their busiest
  • commute, walk or exercise in parks or other green spaces, if you are not affected by pollen
  • if you exercise indoors, make sure the room is well ventilated

Indoor air pollution

The air quality indoors is not always better than outdoors.

There are specific actions you can take to improve your indoor air quality. Consider the following actions:

  • avoid smoking or vaping indoors
  • ventilate when cooking – for example, use an extractor fan or open windows
  • check labels on cleaning products, aerosols and paints – if you need to use these products indoors follow the manufacturer's instructions and use in a ventilated area
  • look for products labelled as low emission – these might be labelled “low VOC”
  • avoid burning on a stove or open fire if you can 

Reduce your contribution to air pollution

Everyone can take action to reduce how much they contribute to air pollution. Consider the following actions:

  • walk, cycle and use public transport more frequently
  • drive less, and when you do drive, drive smarter – car share if you can, avoid idling, and keep your vehicle well maintained
  • consider switching to a hybrid or electric vehicle
  • avoid unnecessary burning at home – for example, using a wood burner or open fire
  • if you need to burn for heating, make sure you use “ready to burn” fuels and keep your appliance clean and maintained
