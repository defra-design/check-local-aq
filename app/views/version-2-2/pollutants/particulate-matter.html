Particulate matter is everything in the air that is not a gas. This includes a wide range of small solid or liquid particles, some of which are toxic.

There are 2 main types of particulate matter (PM), which can have different health effects:

  • fine particles (PM2.5) are 2.5 micrometres wide, or smaller
  • coarse particles (PM10) are 10 micrometres wide, or smaller (this includes PM2.5)

For context, the width of a human hair is about 50 to 70 micrometres.

Health effects of particulate matter

Particulate matter is small enough to enter the bloodstream through the lungs and travel around the body, affecting the heart, brain and other organs.

When there is a lot of particulate matter in the air, it can cause coughing, irritation of the eyes, nose and throat, and other short term health effects. People with lung conditions, children and older people are most at risk.

Long term exposure to particulate matter is linked to:

  • asthma and other respiratory conditions
  • lung cancer and lung tissue damage
  • chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)
  • heart disease and heart failure
  • strokes
  • Alzheimer's disease
  • Parkinson's disease

Where particulate matter comes from

Particulate matter can come from natural processes, vehicle emissions, industrial processes and other human activity. It includes smoke, dust, soot, and smog.

The main sources of particulate matter pollution are:

  • fuel burning by cars and other vehicles
  • fuel burning by industry, such as biomass or coal
  • fuel burning by households, such as indoor wood burners
  • vehicle tyres and brakes getting worn down
  • dust and soil from agriculture, construction, landfills and quarrying
  • sea spray particles
  • burning vegetation and wildfires
  • pollen

Short term high levels of particulate matter can have many causes, for example:

  • lots of road traffic, roadworks or traffic jams
  • major construction or agricultural activity
  • wind, rain and other weather conditions
  • high pollen count at certain times of year, like spring and summer

How particulate matter is measured

The government has specific targets for reducing PM2.5 emissions and keeping levels low. Reducing PM2.5 will also reduce PM10, because they come from many of the same sources.

Find out how air pollution is measured in the UK.

Measuring PM2.5

PM2.5 pollution is considered:

  • low from 0 to 35 micrograms per cubic metre (µg/m³)
  • moderate from 36 to 53 µg/m³
  • high from 54 to 70 µg/m³
  • very high at 71 µg/m³ or higher

Measuring PM10

PM10 pollution is considered:

  • low from 0 to 50 micrograms per cubic metre (µg/m³)
  • moderate from 51 to 75 µg/m³
  • high from 76 to 100 µg/m³
  • very high at 101 µg/m³ or higher
